# CPDR Lab Review: Strategic Relevance to Oncovera

I reviewed the six laboratories listed by CPDR—Ellis, Shafi, Su, Schafer, Tan, and Shrestha—along with CPDR’s Epidemiology Research Program and biorepository. 

## Executive assessment
CPDR could be an unusually strong partner for Oncovera because its capabilities span the entire translational chain:

**Patient and biospecimen resources → tissue and spatial biology → mechanistic models → computational integration → clinically meaningful biomarkers and treatment decisions.**

CPDR maintains a multicenter clinical database and a repository containing prostate tissue, blood, body fluids, and derived materials from men with and without prostate cancer treated within the military health system. Its epidemiology group also supports outcomes research, survivorship studies, study design, and statistical analysis. 

The best strategy is therefore not six separate laboratory collaborations. It is a coordinated **CPDR–Oncovera Longitudinal Prostate Cancer Intelligence Program**, with selected labs contributing specialized components.

---

## 1. Shrestha Lab  
### Computational cancer genomics, multi-omics and fragmentomics

The Shrestha Lab is probably the strongest direct match for Oncovera Intelligence. Its work integrates genomic, transcriptomic, proteomic, epigenomic, and clinical data using computational biology, systems biology, machine learning, and liquid-biopsy fragmentomics. 

Recent lab-listed research includes:

- Genome-scale CRISPR studies identifying PTGES3 as a direct regulator of androgen-receptor function  
- Extracellular-vesicle delivery of CRISPR constructs targeting AR in castration-resistant prostate cancer  
- Genomic and transcriptomic features associated with resistance to androgen-receptor signaling inhibitors  
- Effects of AR inhibition on MHC-I expression and immune recognition  
- Accessible-chromatin and three-dimensional-genome atlases of advanced prostate cancer  
- Alternative promoter usage during progression and treatment resistance  

### Why this matters to Oncovera
This lab could help construct the computational foundation for an Oncovera disease-state model spanning:

**Localized disease → biochemical recurrence → ADT response → treatment resistance → CRPC and metastatic progression.**

Particularly promising opportunities include:

- Joint development of fragmentomic and cfDNA models  
- Integration of ctDNA, CTC, extracellular-vesicle, transcriptomic, spatial, and clinical data  
- Identification of features that predict ARSI resistance before conventional progression  
- Building interpretable patient-specific disease-state graphs for the Oncovera provider portal  
- Distinguishing prognostic biomarkers from genuinely actionable treatment-response biomarkers  

**Recommended role:** Computational and multi-omic co-development lead.

## 2. Tan Lab  
### Integrated oncogenomics, spatial profiling and precision medicine

The Tan Lab combines genomic signatures, proteogenomics, digital pathology, spatial profiling, tumor heterogeneity, and deep learning. The group is particularly interested in predicting metastatic progression and survival by integrating pathology, molecular data, and clinicopathologic variables. 

The laboratory has contributed to work involving:

- TMPRSS2–ERG detection and molecular stratification  
- Development and application of ERG-related diagnostic reagents  
- Annexin A2 as a prognostic marker  
- Focal p53 abnormalities and lymphovascular invasion as predictors of metastatic progression  
- Germline DNA-repair alterations, including findings relevant to African American men  
- Digital spatial analysis of cancer-driver and immune markers  

### Why this matters to Oncovera
Tan’s program closely matches Oncovera’s goal of turning heterogeneous information into clinically useful patient profiles. The lab could contribute:

- Multimodal risk models combining histology, spatial biomarkers, genomics, proteomics, and clinical outcomes  
- Development of an Oncovera metastatic-transition risk score  
- Validation of pathology-derived features against blood- or urine-based assays  
- Ancestry-aware model development and bias testing  
- Conversion of complex research findings into deployable IHC or pathology-based assays  

**Recommended role:** Multimodal clinical prediction and digital-pathology lead.

## 3. Schafer Lab  
### Spatial immunology, proteogenomics and immune biomarkers

The Schafer Lab studies proteogenomic drivers of prostate cancer and the spatial and temporal relationships between tumor cells and the immune microenvironment. Its current work includes T-cell and B-cell repertoires, tumor antigens, ancestry, and progression. 

Relevant publications and research directions include:

- Mast-cell depletion as a marker associated with prostate cancer progression in a military cohort  
- Focal p53 and lymphovascular invasion as predictors of metastatic progression  
- Prostate cancer autoantibodies  
- AI-assisted pathology grading  
- Integrated proteogenomic investigations of shared and ancestry-associated drivers of localized prostate cancer; some recent items remain preprints rather than completed peer-reviewed studies  

### Why this matters to Oncovera
Schafer’s work could give Oncovera a much richer immune and microenvironmental dimension. Opportunities include:

- An Oncovera immune-state and immune-exclusion score  
- Identification of circulating immune, protein, or autoantibody surrogates of tissue findings  
- Correlation of spatial T-cell/B-cell states with biochemical recurrence and treatment response  
- Selection of tumor-associated proteins and antigens for Oncovera’s aptamer-discovery program  
- Ancestry-aware validation of immune and proteomic biomarkers  
- Potential identification of targets for imaging, vaccine, radioligand, or targeted-delivery programs  

**Recommended role:** Spatial immune biology and antigen/target-discovery lead.

## 4. Ellis Lab  
### Epigenetics, lineage plasticity and therapeutic resistance

The Ellis Lab investigates cancer epigenetics, cellular plasticity, tumor evolution, and resistance. Its platforms include organoids, genetically engineered models, CRISPR, single-cell sequencing, and spatial transcriptomic and epigenomic methods. 

The most relevant publication threads include:

- Cooperation between RB1 and TP53 loss in lineage plasticity, metastasis, and antiandrogen resistance  
- SOX2-driven plasticity in TP53/RB1-deficient prostate cancer  
- TOP2A and EZH2 as markers of aggressive disease  
- Reactivation of developmental epigenomic programs during metastatic progression  
- EZH2 inhibition, innate immune signaling, and potential sensitization to immunotherapy  
- MYBL2 and CDK2 vulnerability in RB1-deficient or neuroendocrine disease  
- Loss of ZFP36/TTP, inflammatory signaling, early recurrence, and lethal progression  

### Why this matters to Oncovera
Ellis offers the mechanistic biology needed to keep Oncovera from becoming merely a pattern-matching company. This work can help define biologically meaningful states such as:

- AR-dependent adenocarcinoma  
- Emerging lineage plasticity  
- Double-negative disease  
- Neuroendocrine transition  
- Inflammatory or cytokine-driven resistance  
- Epigenetically mediated treatment escape  

A particularly valuable project would seek blood, CTC, extracellular-vesicle, or cfDNA surrogates of tissue-defined plasticity states involving RB1/TP53, SOX2, MYBL2, EZH2, and ZFP36.

**Recommended role:** Mechanistic resistance and lineage-plasticity lead.
